How Others See Me

As part of this semester's coursework, I collected feedback from five peers using a Behavioral Communication Style survey. The survey measures how others perceive your communication across two dimensions: proactivity (how assertive and forward-leaning you are) and reactivity (how emotionally expressive you are).

The results surprised me. Four out of five peers rated me as an Expressive communicator, and one rated me as a Driver.

Expressive

High proactivity, high reactivity. Energetic, enthusiastic, and open about sharing ideas. Communicates with confidence and emotion.

Driver

High proactivity, low reactivity. Direct, decisive, and results-oriented. Focused on efficiency over emotional expression.

Analytical

Low proactivity, low reactivity. Careful, methodical, and detail-oriented. Prefers data and logic. This is where I placed myself.

The Gap Between Identity and Reputation

I went into this semester seeing myself as more reserved. Focused on the work, logic first, head down. I figured if I just did good work, that would be enough. But with all the hardships and issues that came up throughout the semester, I realized that doing good work quietly only gets you so far. Sometimes you have to speak up. Sometimes you have to step into the uncomfortable part.

So I started pushing myself out of my comfort zone. Being more proactive. Expressing myself more. Being more confident in sharing my ideas, even when I wasn't sure how they'd land. And being kind, not just to my teammates, but to myself when things didn't go perfectly.

It was a hard first step to take, but it was an important one. I still make mistakes. I still run into conflict and criticism. But instead of letting those things stop me, I've learned to accept them and use them to grow. Every difficult conversation, every piece of feedback I didn't want to hear, it all feeds into who I'm becoming. And that's what fuels where I'm going next.

What My Team Taught Me

Over the course of the semester, my team went through three rounds of evaluations. Each round gave us a chance to reflect on how we were working together and where we could do better.

The consistent feedback was encouraging. My effort and quality of work were above the team average every round, and I was recognized as the highest contributor with a weighted contribution score of 7. That felt good to see, because those are the things I care about most. I want the work I put out to be solid and I want people to know I'm giving it everything I've got.

But the growth areas were just as real. I scored below the team average in punctuality, active listening, and being open to opinions that were different from mine. I also scored lower in holding teammates accountable, which is something I've always struggled with. I tend to just pick up the slack myself rather than having the harder conversation.

Those aren't easy things to read about yourself. But I'm glad I have them. You can't fix what you don't see, and now I know what to work on.

Where I'm Growing

The feedback I got this semester pointed to some specific things I need to work on. My confidence can sometimes come across as intensity, and I tend to rush through presentations instead of giving people time to absorb things. My team evaluations also showed that my deliverables needed more polish than they should have, and I wasn't always as punctual or prepared for meetings as I could have been.

To work on this, I've started being more deliberate about slowing down, both in how I present and how I prepare. I'm giving myself more time to review my work before handing it off, and I'm making a conscious effort to show up to meetings earlier and more ready to contribute. I'm also practicing pausing more in conversations, making sure I'm listening and not just waiting for my turn to talk.

None of this is fixed yet. But I see it now, and that feels like the right place to start.

Sony Database Advising Group

Type: Database Design Role: Team member (6-person group)

Our team designed a relational database system for Sony's customer service operations. The system had to handle a lot: customer accounts, product purchases, warranties, service requests, repairs, replacements, returns, and delivery logistics. Every piece had to connect to the others in a way that actually worked.

What I Did

My focus was on the technical side. I helped build the ER model, mapping out all the entities, attributes, and relationships, then handled the translation from the conceptual schema into relational notation. That meant defining the foreign keys, working through the cardinalities, and making sure things like employee subclass inheritance and the one-to-one warranty relationship were set up correctly. While I worked on the database design, my teammates put the report together.

What It Shows

This is the kind of work I enjoy most. Taking a messy business problem, figuring out how all the pieces relate to each other, and building a structure that holds up. My teammates handled the documentation, and I got to do the part I'm drawn to: the building.

Amazon Online Profitability Analysis

Type: Data Analysis & Regression Modeling Role: Team member (5-person group)

Our team analyzed about 10,000 historical Amazon orders across Office Supplies, Furniture, and Technology to figure out what drives profit per item. The goal was to build a regression model that could predict profitability and point to the variables that matter most.

What I Did

I was the one working with the raw data. I cleaned it up so the Tableau side of the team had something usable to visualize, then dug into the dataset myself to understand what we were actually working with. From there, I focused on building the regression model, testing different combinations of variables, creating dummy variables for things like shipping mode and product category, and running through five iterations to find the best fit we could get with the data we had. My teammates took what I built and put the report together.

What It Shows

The final model only explained about 27% of the variation in profit, which honestly told us something important: the dataset was missing key cost variables like shipping costs and fulfillment expenses. Knowing when a model falls short and being honest about why is just as valuable as building one that works perfectly. That's the Bedrock side of how I work. I'd rather give you the real picture than make the numbers look better than they are.
